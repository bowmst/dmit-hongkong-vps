## DMIT's Three Network Tiers Explained

Understanding which DMIT Hong Kong VPS plan to buy requires understanding their three-tier network structure. Each tier isn't just a price bracket — it's a fundamentally different network path.

### Premium Network (HKG.Pro) — CN2 GIA Triple Carrier

This is DMIT's flagship routing tier for China-facing workloads. Premium uses bidirectional CN2 GIA for China Telecom, premium direct lines for China Unicom, and CMI (China Mobile International) direct connections.

What does that mean in practice? CN2 GIA (Global Internet Access) is China Telecom's dedicated backbone — a separate, higher-priority network that doesn't share congestion with the standard ChinaNet. During peak hours when standard routing degrades, CN2 GIA holds its performance.

Premium is the right choice when latency and routing stability during peak China internet hours are non-negotiable. E-commerce, SaaS products serving Chinese enterprise clients, live streaming infrastructure — these workloads belong on Premium.

### Eyeball Network (HKG.EB) — CMI Bidirectional + Unicom/Telecom Return

Eyeball is the middle ground — significantly more affordable than Premium while still delivering meaningful optimization for mainland Chinese users. It uses China Mobile International (CMI) for bidirectional routing, with Unicom and Telecom as return paths.

The experience is noticeably better than generic international routing and more than adequate for most content websites, developer tools, and SaaS applications that primarily serve Chinese audiences without extreme latency sensitivity.

Eyeball's bigger traffic allocations and lower price point make it the volume play — if you need to move serious amounts of data to and from mainland China without the Premium price tag, Eyeball is worth a hard look.

### Tier 1 Network (HKG.T1) — RETN International Transit

Tier 1 uses RETN, an international IP transit provider optimized for Europe-Asia connectivity. This isn't a China-optimized route — it's a clean, high-capacity international backbone.

The audience for HKG.T1 is global businesses that happen to want a Hong Kong presence: international CDN nodes, global developer tools, applications serving Southeast Asia and Australia, or any workload where the Hong Kong geographic location matters more than mainland China latency.

The value proposition is remarkable: the WEE entry plan starts at $36.90 per year, with 1Gbps uplinks and 1TB of traffic. For an international-facing server at that price point, it's genuinely hard to beat.
